Offshore Wind Energy Siting

Anti-Correlated Sub-Cluster Portfolio Optimization for Offshore Wind

Finding regions where geographic diversification maximally stabilizes wind power output across seasons

01 — PROBLEM

The Intermittency Problem

Wind power output varies dramatically by season and location. A single site can swing 3× between peak and trough months.

  • Offshore wind is the highest-density renewable resource in the U.S., but seasonal variance makes grid integration expensive
  • Industry picks sites by mean wind speed alone — ignoring the portfolio effect of geographic spread
  • Can we find clusters of sites whose seasonal output profiles are anti-correlated, reducing aggregate variance?
  • Wind Power Density (WPD) = ½ρv³ — captures both wind speed and air density in a single metric (W/m²)

Hypothesis: Within each U.S. coastal region, sub-clusters of sites exist with negatively correlated monthly wind power density profiles. A Markowitz-style portfolio allocation across these sub-clusters can reduce output variance by >30% vs. the best single sub-cluster.

05 — METHOD

Model Design: Sub-Cluster Portfolio Optimization

  • Region Assignment: Golden-zone cells assigned to 6 U.S. coastal regions (Alaska, Pacific, Gulf, Southeast, Mid-Atlantic, New England)
  • Monthly WPD Extraction: For each cell, extract 12-month WPD time series via DuckDB spatial join across all monthly parquets
  • Correlation-Based Subclustering: Hierarchical clustering on pairwise correlation distance (d = 1 − ρ) with Ward linkage → 3 sub-clusters per region
  • Markowitz Optimization: Mean-variance portfolio across sub-cluster monthly profiles, long-only weights summing to 1

Key insight: We treat sub-clusters as "assets" with monthly WPD as "returns." Anti-correlated sub-clusters provide natural hedging — when one area has low wind, another has high wind.

07 — INTERPRETATION

Why the Pacific is Unique

The only region with genuinely anti-correlated sub-clusters (ρ = −0.30)

All other regions have minimum sub-cluster correlations above +0.33, meaning their seasonal profiles move together. The Pacific's sub-clusters actively offset each other.

  • Pacific NW (WA/OR): Dominant winter-peak pattern driven by North Pacific storm track
  • N. California: Transition zone with summer-peak from thermal low pressure — opposite seasonal phase
  • S. California: Moderate year-round profile, low variance — acts as portfolio stabilizer
  • Result: Allocating 55.6% PNW + 10.4% NorCal + 33.9% SoCal cuts output variance by 70.6%

Business impact: 70.6% variance reduction → more predictable revenue streams → lower cost of capital for project financing. In offshore wind, reducing output uncertainty by even 10% can lower the weighted average cost of capital by 50–100 bps — worth hundreds of millions over a project's 25-year life.

08 — NEXT STEPS

Limitations & Next Steps

Limitations

  • Single year of data (2025) — multi-year analysis needed for climate robustness
  • Monthly temporal resolution loses intra-day and weekly variance patterns
  • No transmission infrastructure, grid connection costs, or permitting constraints
  • Uniform 3-subcluster count — optimal K may vary by region

Next Steps

  • Extend to 10+ year ERA5 archive for out-of-sample validation
  • Hourly portfolio optimization to capture diurnal anti-correlation
  • Add LCOE (levelized cost) as a constraint to the portfolio model
  • Cross-region portfolio: combine Pacific + Alaska for continental-scale hedging

Core takeaway: Geographic diversification within a single wind region can reduce output variance by up to 70% — a fundamentally different approach to wind farm siting that borrows from financial portfolio theory.
